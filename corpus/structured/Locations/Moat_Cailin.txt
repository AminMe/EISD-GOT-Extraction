<h2 data-rte-empty-lines-before="1">Behind the scenes</h2>
<ul><li data-rte-spaces-before="1">Renly pronounces the name "Moat Cait-lin" the one time he mentions it in Season 2, but the Boltons pronounce it correctly as "Cay-lin" in Season 4.
</li></ul>
<ul data-rte-empty-lines-before="1"><li data-rte-spaces-before="1">Renly's offer would've involved the Starks losing a substantial amount of their pre-war territory on <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"the Neck","link":"the Neck","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[the Neck]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/The_Neck" title="The Neck">the Neck</a>, including <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"Greywater Watch","link":"Greywater Watch","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[Greywater Watch]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/Greywater_Watch" title="Greywater Watch">Greywater Watch</a>, but Catelyn does not mention this. Possibly it was a script error. It may also be possible that Renly has only a simplified knowledge of geography of the North, or that he was speaking vaguely and considered "the North" to <i>symbolically</i> start at Moat Cailin, because of the strategical importance of the fortress, and meant leaving the North's territory as it was.<img data-rte-meta="{"type":"ext","placeholder":1,"wikitext":"<ref name=\"E14\"\/>"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" class="placeholder placeholder-ext" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw==" type="ext" />
</li></ul>

<h2 data-rte-empty-lines-before="1">In the books</h2>
<p data-rte-fromparser="true">In the <i><a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"A Song of Ice and Fire","link":"A Song of Ice and Fire","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[A Song of Ice and Fire]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/A_Song_of_Ice_and_Fire" title="A Song of Ice and Fire">A Song of Ice and Fire</a></i> novels, Moat Cailin is said to have been built well over ten thousand years ago by the <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"Children of the Forest","link":"Children of the Forest","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[Children of the Forest]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Forest" title="Children of the Forest">Children of the Forest</a>, though the accuracy of this is unclear. The fortress commands the northern end of the causeway which carries the <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"Kingsroad","link":"Kingsroad","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[Kingsroad]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/Kingsroad" title="Kingsroad">Kingsroad</a> through the bogs and swamps of the Neck, at a point in <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"Westeros","link":"Westeros","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[Westeros]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/Westeros" title="Westeros">Westeros</a> where the swamps extend almost from coast to coast. Thus, any large host has to pass the fortress to enter the North. Due to the placement of the three surviving towers around the bottleneck and with no firm ground to deploy siege equipment to the south, a few hundred archers with sufficient ammunition could hold off a much larger army for some time from Moat Cailin. Moat Cailin was one of the vital reasons why the First Men were able to successfully resist the <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"Andals","link":"Andals","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[Andals]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/Andals" title="Andals">Andals</a>' attempts to invade the North as they did the rest of Westeros to the south.
</p><p data-rte-fromparser="true" data-rte-empty-lines-before="1">Also according to myth, the Children attempted to use Moat Cailin to hold back the invading <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"First Men","link":"First Men","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[First Men]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/First_Men" title="First Men">First Men</a> and, when that failed due to the humans' superior numbers, attempted to shatter the Neck and completely separate the North from the South in the same manner they shattered the Arm of <a data-rte-meta="{"type":"internal","text":"Dorne","link":"Dorne","wasblank":true,"noforce":true,"wikitext":"[[Dorne]]"}" data-rte-instance="130814-17958452356cca85a6c90c" href="/wiki/Dorne" title="Dorne">Dorne</a> centuries earlier. However, the Children failed and only succeeded in flooding it, creating bogs and swamps. However, the cataclysm proved the strength of their power and may have proved instrumental in bringing the First Men to agree to the terms of the Pact that ended hostilities between the two races.
</p><p data-rte-fromparser="true" data-rte-empty-lines-before="1">While in the present era most of Moat Cailin's former towers have fallen into ruin, even the three remaining towers are more than capable of defending the passage to the North, provided that they are fully manned. A key point is that Moat Cailin was only designed to resist attack from the south, and thus its northern flank is relatively exposed to attack by even a small force.
</p>
